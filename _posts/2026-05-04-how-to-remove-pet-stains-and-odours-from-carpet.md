## Act Fast: The First Five Minutes Matter

Speed is everything with pet stains. Urine begins soaking through carpet fibres and into the underpad within minutes. The longer it sits, the harder it is to remove — and the worse it smells.

**Here's what to do immediately:**

1. **Blot, don't rub.** Grab a stack of clean white cloths or paper towels and press firmly into the stain. Rubbing spreads the urine outward and pushes it deeper into the fibres. Blot from the outside edges inward.
2. **Keep blotting until the towels come away nearly dry.** You may need to go through quite a few. Stand on the towels if you need more pressure.
3. **Rinse with cool water.** Pour a small amount of cool water over the spot and blot again. This dilutes the urine before it sets.

What you do in these first few minutes makes a bigger difference than any cleaning product you apply later.

## Why Enzymatic Cleaners Are Your Best Friend

Regular carpet cleaners mask the smell — enzymatic cleaners actually eliminate it. Here's the difference: pet urine contains uric acid crystals that bind to carpet fibres and resist most soaps and detergents. Standard cleaners may remove the visible stain, but those crystals remain. On humid days (and we get plenty of those on the coast), moisture reactivates those crystals and the smell comes back.

Enzymatic cleaners use biological enzymes that break down uric acid at the molecular level. They're the only reliable way to fully neutralize pet odour from carpet.

### How to use an enzymatic cleaner properly

- Blot up as much urine as possible first.
- Saturate the stain with the enzymatic cleaner — you need the product to reach as deep as the urine did.
- Cover the area with a damp cloth and leave it for the time specified on the label (usually 10–15 minutes, sometimes longer).
- Blot dry and allow to air dry completely.

Look for enzymatic cleaners at your local pet store. Nature's Miracle and Rocco & Roxie are both widely available and effective. Make sure the product specifically says "enzymatic" on the label.

## DIY Methods That Work — and Ones That Make It Worse

If you're learning how to remove pet stains from carpet with household products, here's what actually helps and what to avoid.

### What works

- **Baking soda:** After cleaning and while still slightly damp, sprinkle a generous layer of baking soda over the area. Let it sit overnight, then vacuum. Baking soda is excellent at absorbing residual odour.
- **White vinegar and water (50/50 mix):** Good for fresh stains as a first-pass treatment before enzymatic cleaner. The mild acidity helps neutralize ammonia in urine.

### What makes it worse

- **Steam cleaning or hot water on fresh stains.** Heat sets protein-based stains like urine. If you hit a fresh pet stain with high heat, you can permanently bond the stain to the carpet fibres. Always use cool or lukewarm water.
- **Ammonia-based cleaners.** Urine contains ammonia. Cleaning with more ammonia can actually encourage your pet to re-mark the same spot — they smell it and think it's a toilet.
- **Scrubbing aggressively.** This damages carpet fibres, spreads the stain, and forces urine deeper into the pad. Always blot.
- **Over-wetting the carpet.** Flooding the area with water or cleaner can cause the urine to spread further into the underpad and subfloor. Use enough product to treat the stain, not drown it.

## When DIY Isn't Enough

Here's the hard truth: if urine has soaked through the carpet into the underpad — which happens with larger accidents, repeat incidents, or stains you didn't catch right away — no amount of surface cleaning will fully remove it. The pad acts like a sponge and holds onto urine long after the carpet above looks clean.

This is especially common with cats. Cat urine is more concentrated than dog urine and has a stronger, more persistent odour. If you've cleaned a spot multiple times and the smell keeps returning, the urine is almost certainly in the pad.

## Preventing Future Accidents

Once you've dealt with the immediate stain, a few habits can help prevent repeats:

- **Use a black light.** A UV flashlight (under $20 at most hardware stores) will reveal old urine stains you can't see. You may find spots you never knew about — and your pet definitely knows they're there.
- **Address the behaviour.** Repeated accidents in the same spot often signal a medical issue, stress, or a litter box problem. Talk to your vet if accidents become frequent.
- **Schedule regular professional cleanings.** Even if your carpets look clean, periodic professional cleaning removes the buildup of dander, tracked-in dirt, and invisible pet residue that accumulates over time.
