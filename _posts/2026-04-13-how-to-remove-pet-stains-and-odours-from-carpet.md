## The First 60 Seconds Matter Most

Speed is your best friend when dealing with a fresh pet accident. The longer urine or vomit sits on carpet fibre, the deeper it soaks — not just into the carpet itself, but through the backing and into the underpad beneath.

Here's what to do immediately:

1. **Blot, don't rub.** Grab clean white towels or paper towels and press firmly into the stain. Rubbing spreads the mess sideways and pushes it deeper into the fibres. Work from the outside edges inward.
2. **Absorb as much liquid as possible.** Stand on the towels if you need to. Swap for fresh ones until no more moisture transfers.
3. **Rinse with cool water.** Pour a small amount of cool (not hot) water onto the spot and blot again. Hot water can set protein-based stains like urine, making them far harder to remove later.
4. **Apply an enzymatic cleaner.** This is the step most people skip — and it's the most important one.

## Why Enzymatic Cleaners Are Non-Negotiable

Most household cleaners mask pet odours with fragrance or use surfactants to lift surface-level stains. They'll make the spot look better for a day or two, but the smell comes back — especially on humid days, which we get plenty of here in Victoria.

Enzymatic cleaners work differently. They contain beneficial bacteria that produce enzymes to break down the uric acid crystals in pet urine at a molecular level. These crystals are what cause that persistent ammonia smell, and they're incredibly stubborn. Regular soap and water won't touch them.

Look for a product specifically labelled "enzymatic" or "bio-enzymatic" at your local pet store. Follow the directions on the bottle — most need to stay damp on the stain for several hours to work properly. Cover the area with a damp cloth to keep it from drying out too fast.

## DIY Methods That Work (and Ones That Make It Worse)

When you're searching for how to remove pet stains from carpet, you'll find no shortage of home remedies. Some are genuinely helpful. Others will cost you more in the long run.

### What Works

- **White vinegar and water (50/50 mix):** A decent first-response cleaner for fresh stains. The acidity helps neutralize ammonia in urine. Apply, let sit for 5–10 minutes, then blot dry.
- **Baking soda:** After treating with vinegar or an enzymatic cleaner, sprinkle baking soda over the area once it's nearly dry. It helps absorb residual odour. Vacuum thoroughly once fully dry.
- **Hydrogen peroxide (3%):** Can work on older, set-in stains. Test on a hidden area first, as it can lighten some carpet dyes.

### What Makes It Worse

- **Steam cleaning or hot water on fresh stains:** Heat bonds protein stains to carpet fibres. If you hit a fresh urine stain with a hot water extraction machine before treating it enzymatically, you can permanently set the stain and lock in the odour.
- **Ammonia-based cleaners:** Urine contains ammonia. Cleaning with more ammonia can actually encourage your pet to mark the same spot again — they smell it as a signal.
- **Over-wetting the carpet:** Flooding the area with water or cleaner can push the stain deeper into the pad and create moisture problems, including mould growth. This is especially important in Victoria's damp climate, where carpets already face higher ambient humidity.

## When DIY Isn't Enough
