## Act Fast: The First Five Minutes Matter

The single most important thing you can do when a pet accident happens is respond quickly. Fresh urine is far easier to deal with than dried, set-in stains.

### Step-by-step for fresh accidents

1. **Blot, don't rub.** Grab clean white cloths or paper towels and press firmly into the spot to absorb as much liquid as possible. Rubbing spreads the stain and pushes urine deeper into the carpet fibres.
2. **Work from the outside in.** This prevents the stain from expanding.
3. **Keep blotting with fresh towels** until no more moisture transfers.
4. **Rinse with cool water.** Pour a small amount of cool water on the area and blot again. Hot water can set protein-based stains, so keep it cool.
5. **Apply your cleaning solution** (more on that below).

If you catch it in time, this process alone can prevent a permanent stain. But if the urine has already dried — or if the spot has been hit more than once — you'll need to go further.

## Why Enzymatic Cleaners Are Your Best Friend

Not all carpet cleaners are created equal when it comes to pet urine. Standard household cleaners might remove the visible stain, but they often leave behind uric acid crystals — the compounds responsible for that lingering odour that comes back on humid days (and here in Victoria, that's most days from October through April).

**Enzymatic cleaners** work differently. They contain beneficial bacteria that produce enzymes to literally break down the uric acid at a molecular level. No uric acid, no odour. Look for products specifically labelled for pet urine and follow the instructions carefully — most need to stay wet on the area for 10 to 15 minutes to work properly.

A few tips:

- **Test in a hidden area first** to make sure it won't discolour your carpet.
- **Don't mix with other cleaners.** Chemical residue can kill the beneficial bacteria before they do their job.
- **Use enough product.** You need the enzymatic cleaner to reach everywhere the urine went, including down into the carpet backing.

## DIY Methods That Work (and Ones That Don't)

When you're searching for how to remove pet stains from carpet, you'll find no shortage of home remedies. Here's what actually holds up.

### What works

- **Enzymatic cleaners** — as discussed above, these are the gold standard for pet urine.
- **Baking soda** — after cleaning and drying a spot, sprinkling baking soda overnight can help absorb residual odour. Vacuum it up the next morning.
- **White vinegar and water (50/50 mix)** — decent for fresh stains. The acidity helps neutralize ammonia in urine. Apply, let sit for five minutes, then blot dry.

### What makes it worse

- **Steam cleaning on your own.** High heat can permanently bond the protein in urine to carpet fibres, locking in the stain and the smell. If you're renting a machine from the hardware store, proceed with caution on pet-stained areas.
- **Ammonia-based cleaners.** Urine contains ammonia. Using an ammonia cleaner is essentially telling your pet, "Yes, this is the bathroom." They'll return to the same spot.
- **Over-wetting the carpet.** Flooding the area with cleaning solution can push urine further into the underpad and even the subfloor, making the problem dramatically worse.

## When DIY Isn't Enough

Here's the hard truth about pet stains: what you see on the surface is only part of the problem.

Urine doesn't just sit on top of the carpet. It soaks through the fibres, through the carpet backing, and into the underpad beneath. In repeat-offence spots, it can even reach the subfloor. That underpad acts like a sponge — it absorbs urine and holds it, slowly releasing odour over time. No amount of surface cleaning can reach it.
